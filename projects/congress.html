How representative are social media trends of outside events and their importance?

The first question in this paper seeks to answer is on the relationship between social media trends and their real-world event implications. First, it needs to be determined if the effect of social media terms used by Congress members fluctuates by year and how this correlates to both political parties and important dates in US politics. Then, these analyses can be applied to smaller subsections of the data to analyze the term effects by political topic sections such as foreign policy, domestic policy, etc. More and more people are using social media as their news source, so how do their interactions with Congressional posts reflect and place importance on real-life events?

The first step in this social media timeline analysis is to determine if there are meaningful changes in the data over time. Using the data-set of the top terms that were associated with the largest engagement, the variable Average Percentage Effect was utilized. Since this represents a summarization of the effect of the term on social media across Twitter and Facebook, it was used as a comprehensive indicator of the social media engagement effect of certain terms for these analyses. This figure grouped the data by year and party, then plotted the terms by year and engagement effect. To better visualize this, a loess regression curve was generated as a fitted line for the data using the year to predict the engagement effect of all the terms for that year for each political party. These figures were then plotted together and are shown below.

The graph above displays the engagement effect distribution across political party and time, as well as summarizes the data by party with a fitted loess regression line. First, the effects that terms have on social media engagement does significantly differ by year, indicating that this inquiry is worth looking further into. Originally, it was thought that the average percent effect of terms would increase around election times, specifically there would be a larger effect for the party that gained more seats in Congress during that election time. The data found that this was not the case. While engagement did rise in 2016, this same distinct spike could not be found in 2018 and 2020 which are other Congressional election years. In addition, in 2016 Donald Trump was elected as president and Republicans did well in Congressional elections, yet Democrats saw more engagement, which would usually indicate a party was successful at generating support for issues. In an election year, it is thought that more engagement would mean more support, however as Democrats lost key components of the election cycle to Republicans, the data refutes this hypothesis. What is interesting from this data is that while the effects by party do not seem to correlate with the elections, they do seem to be affected by each other. The average social media effects for the two parties move in a seemingly cyclical pattern. While more data would be needed in order to analyze this, the cyclical pattern may be indicative of these rises marking important dates in politics. One example would be the Democratic outlier in 2016 with the term NoBillNoBreak where the Congress Democrats held a unique filibuster for a mass shooting. For every major divisive issue in American politics, a party has one side and the other has a different side, so the engagement effects of each term would cancel each other out by being incredibly close. Therefore, these events are those that are singular and do not have a significant counterpart, meaning that they are unique political dates that can be tracked through social media engagement.

As seen with the data and analysis, these political terms fluctuate with engagement over time as a whole, but a further investigation can be done into whether the yearly term trends are effective in tracking important dates for different political topic veins as well. Using the same methodology as before, the data were plotted with a loess regression model for best fit, however, this time only terms relating to foreign policy were used.

Within the above graph of terms engagement by party and year, some similar trends do appear for this foreign policy subsection of politics. While there is not enough data to make any substantial claims about the trends, this does again single out a set of specific events outside of the models confidence bands that are unique and important within foreign policy in the US. This datas outlier was Republicans using the term Adam Schiff. He was a Congress member who led the impeachment of Trump following the Trump-Ukraine political scandal and the Russia probe, which are large foreign affairs issues. The Republican base started mobilizing around trying to discredit him and get him to resign, making their engagement with the term increase significantly when Republican Congress members tweeted or posted about him. As theorized earlier, impeachment is an issue that was talked about by both sides and had similar effects over an extended period of time, but Adam Schiff specifically represented a peak important event within foreign policy in the US.

In these investigations, the question of if social media could be used to track and quantify important events was answered with social media data of US Congress members. The data concluded that the terms do differ over time, proving that terms can be used to track what is trending in politics at different points in time. While the engagement does not match up to elections, there does appear to be a cyclical pattern between the two parties engagement with their terms, however more data would be necessary for a full analysis. But, this data can be manipulated to track political trends for different sections of politics, as shown through the analysis of foreign policy. In the future, more research could be done by analyzing other groups such as domestic policy, human rights, etc. Overall, social media used by Congress members and the engagement their constituents show can be used to identify and single out specific political issues / dates and their importance in American politics.

How is US partisanship exhibited in social media?

The second question this paper seeks to answer is how American partisanship is exhibited in social media. First, the presence of partisanship in social media usage will need to be proven. This alleged partisanship will then need to be identified, validated, and quantified to form a full analysis of its presence and effect in social media. These analyses were done using data for frequently used terms by Congress members on Twitter and Facebook. These sites claim to be reflective of our society, but can they be used to analyse US government opinions as well?

To begin, there needs to be a way to identify if there is partisanship present in the social media accounts of the Congress members. Using the dataset of terms that were used most exclusively by members of each party, the variable Positive-to-Negative Ratio was used. It represents a ratio of how much engagement by each term differed when it was used by different parties. The ratio data were then aggregated into a histogram that plotted the frequency of each ratio for the data-set. These data were additionally separated by the political party that used the term more often to produce comparable histograms. The mean value for all of the ratios was then found and plotted on the histogram. The resulting histogram is displayed below.

The graph above displays the frequency of terms Positive-to-Negative ratio values, separated by party. The data for both parties are skewed by having values that have a large ratio value outside of a normal distribution. These values may indicate terms that are extreme in their partisanship due to their significantly larger effect when used by their one specific party, a quality that is defined in this paper by the term hyperpartisan. Outside of the similar skew, the data reveals that there is a visual difference in the histograms for the Democratic terms and the predominantly Republican ones. On average, there seems to be more social media favorability for Democrats in Congress. This could indicate that Democrats use social media more effectively than Republicans, or have terms that mobilize their base more. According to the overall mean Positive-to-Negative ratio, the Republicans saw the bulk of their terms below the line and therefore had comparatively less effective term use on average than Democrats. Due to this term effect and usage difference between the two political parties, it is now evident that there is partisanship present in the social media accounts of US Congress members.

While the last analysis displayed that there is partisanship present in social media, this analysis can get deeper and pinpoint this partisanship. Using the same terms data as above, a new variable to measure partisanship was created. For each term this Party Difference variable represents a difference in term usage by political party. The top ten terms for each party with the largest party difference variable value were aggregated with their color representing the party that used the term most frequently. These top terms were arranged based on their party difference and are represented below as a graph and as a table.

The graph and table above show a representation of the terms that have the largest party difference. Democrats tended to have larger values than the Republican used terms, meaning that as a party they tend to use more of the same terminology in social media. This could indicate better party cohesion for Democrats due to the large proportion of members that chose to use the same terms. It is also important to note that this high Party Difference value means that a large number of the terms own party used it in social media, as well as a small number of the opposing party using it. Therefore, by having a larger gap in party usage, this variable quantifies and identifies how partisan a term is in social media. The terms with the six largest party difference values were equal pay, pro growth, gun safety, lgbt, bureaucrat, and worker right. As discussed before, that would make these specific terms hyper-partisan terms due to them being used near exclusively by one party. To verify this, the most partisan term the data found was equal pay. This is a large platform issue for Democrats and the Congressional lawmakers have drafted a lot of legislation around the issue, whereas the Republican members have not, therefore validating the results. Similarly for the other terms, these all do tend to be issues that America is deeply divided on by political party. The data then can show this deep division and pinpoint key areas through social media usage by Congress members.

In the question of US partisanship in social media, this Congressional dataset was able to offer many insights. By looking into the difference in effects of the terms each party used, the data show that it is possible to view partisanship in American politics through the social media activities of the legislative branch members. The analysis was then able to narrow down and even quantify this partisanship by term to single out key divisive issues in the American political landscape. Overall, it is apparent that the Democratically used terms had more of an effect when used on social media. It is also evident that Democrats tend to use more partisan terms than Republicans. This data and analysis shows that US partisanship can be shown, identified, and quantified exclusively through the social media usage of Congress members.
